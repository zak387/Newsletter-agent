# Newsletter Reference Library

A curated set of strong newsletter examples across formats. Use this as a craft reference when drafting — study the *moves*, not the surface. Do not copy voice; voice always comes from the creator's Block 1 profile.

---

## How to use this file

When drafting a newsletter in Block 3:
1. Identify the confirmed format (Personal Letter / Roundup / Curation / Deep Dive / Trends).
2. Read the matching section below.
3. Pay attention to the annotated "what's working" notes — those are the transferable moves.
4. Apply the moves through the creator's voice profile. Never borrow phrasing.

## Personal Letter

Best-in-class practitioners: Justin Welsh, Sahil Bloom, Jay Clouse, Nathan Barry, Jesse Pujji.

### Example 1 — Jay Clouse, "I can't believe I almost bailed on this..."

**Opening move:** Drops the reader mid-scene. "I spent the first half of this week in Franklin, Tennessee." Concrete place, concrete time, no throat-clearing.

**Emotional arc:** Excitement → creeping anxiety → temptation to bail → sticking it out → relief and insight. The reader rides the arc with him.

**Structural move:** Two stories stitched together — the mastermind trip and the improv "count to 20" game — with a thematic bridge ("two jobs: speaking and not speaking"). The second story earns the first story's meaning.

**Sign-off:** Warm, low-pressure CTA to The Lab. Not the point of the email, just a door left open. PS points to the podcast.

**What to steal:**
- Start in a specific scene, not a thesis.
- Name the fear out loud — it builds trust faster than any credential.
- Use a second, unrelated story as the lens that reframes the first.
- Keep the CTA proportional to the story's emotional weight.

### Example 2 — Jay Clouse, "You give better advice than you take."

**Opening move:** A confession quoted from his own AI assistant. Self-deprecation as a hook earns attention because it's rare and specific.

**Structural move:** Problem stated → three numbered gaps (narrow platform focus, delegate more, reposition promise). Each gap is personal but generalizable — the reader can map their own version onto it.

**Voice tells:** Uses second-person ("Let me be clear: You can compete on any platform you want") to pivot from confession to teaching without losing intimacy.

**What to steal:**
- Turn a private realization into a public framework.
- Number the sub-points so the reader can hold them.
- Pivot from "I" to "you" only after you've earned it with vulnerability.

### Example 3 — Justin Welsh pattern (generalized)

Justin's hallmark moves:
- Subject lines that tee up a specific lesson ("What I learned after [thing]").
- Short paragraphs — often one sentence.
- Open loops early, close them late.
- Clean section dividers, never walls of text.
- One hard-earned takeaway per issue. No padding.

## Roundup

Best-in-class: Morning Brew, The Hustle, The Rundown AI.

### Moves that define the format
- **Opening is a vibe, not a summary.** 2–3 lines of voice before any news. The Brew opens with a joke; Rundown AI opens with a crisp context line.
- **Each story is skimmable in 10 seconds.** Headline → 1-sentence TL;DR → 2–4 bullet takeaways → one opinionated line from the editor.
- **The editor's take is the product.** Without it, it's an RSS feed. The take should be specific, not "this is interesting."
- **Quick Hits earns its name.** Link + 6–10 word context. Nothing longer.
- **No emoji clutter, no hype words.** Clarity beats cleverness.

### Anti-patterns
- Burying the lede under a long personal intro — wrong format for that.
- Takes that just restate the TL;DR in different words.
- Bullet points with no parallel structure.

## Curation

Best-in-class: James Clear 3-2-1, Marketing Max, Dense Discovery.

### James Clear 3-2-1 — why it works
- **Constraint creates craft.** 3 ideas, 2 quotes, 1 question. The format forces compression.
- **Ideas are original, not summaries.** Each idea is a self-contained aphorism you could screenshot.
- **Quotes are thematically linked** to the ideas — not random. They reinforce, they don't decorate.
- **The question lingers.** It's not rhetorical; it invites a pause. "What am I optimizing for that I never chose?"
- **Zero CTA pressure.** The trust compounds because the format is the product.

### What to steal
- Ruthless compression. Cut every word that isn't load-bearing.
- Ideas should pass the screenshot test — would someone share this as an image?
- Quotes must earn their spot. If it's a filler quote, delete it.

## Deep Dive (reference only — not a Block 2 format here)

Best-in-class: Lenny Rachitsky, Stratechery, Not Boring (Packy McCormick).

### Moves
- **One question per issue, fully answered.** The title is often literally a question.
- **Earn the length.** Every section should be a mini-essay that could almost stand alone.
- **Diagrams, tables, and pull-quotes** break up the wall of text.
- **Closing synthesis.** The last section compresses the whole argument into something portable.

## Subject line patterns that work

Across formats, these subject line frames recur in top performers:

1. **Confession / self-deprecation** — "I can't believe I almost bailed on this..."
2. **Curiosity gap** — "Your mission, if you choose to accept..."
3. **Quoted insight** — "'You give better advice than you take.'"
4. **Direct promise** — "A huge opportunity for creators right now"
5. **Named mechanism** — "The 3-part framework I use for [X]"
6. **Question** — "Ready to double down on YouTube?"
7. **Contrarian take** — "Why [accepted wisdom] is wrong"

Each should be paired with a preview text that *extends* the subject, not repeats it. Preview text is the second punch.

## CTA craft notes

- **One primary CTA per issue.** The reader should not have to scroll to find it.
- **The P.S. is the second swing.** Often converts better than the main CTA because readers skim to the bottom.
- **Match CTA energy to issue energy.** A vulnerable personal letter should not end with a hard sales ask — use a soft community or content CTA instead.
- **Name the specific next action.** Not "learn more" — "Book a free discovery call" or "Click here to join us."

---

## Universal rules (apply to every format)

- No emojis unless the creator's Block 1 profile explicitly uses them.
- No AI tells: "dive into," "unpack," "in today's fast-paced world," "game-changer," "leverage," "it's important to note," em-dash clusters, tricolons for decoration.
- Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph. Whitespace is a feature.
- Specificity beats abstraction every time. Proper nouns, numbers, scenes.
- Read it out loud before shipping. If you stumble, rewrite the line.
- The reader is busy, smart, and skeptical. Earn every sentence.
