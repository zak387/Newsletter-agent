## She was reading the wrong side of the box

Last Tuesday I was in the snack aisle at a local Target. A woman next to me was holding a box of crackers with a green leaf on the front and the words "made with real vegetables" in big letters. She was reading the nutrition panel. Calories, fat, sodium, protein. She put it in her cart.

I glanced at the ingredient list on the back. Soybean oil was the third ingredient. Sunflower oil was the fifth.

She was reading the wrong side of the box.

That is the blind spot I want to talk about today. Not because she did something wrong. Because she did what almost everyone does, and the box was designed to let her do it.

## Eight out of ten products on the shelf have seed oils in them

Including the stuff marketed to families like yours. Clean fonts, leafy packaging, words like wholesome on the front. Seed oils are the cheapest fat a food company can use. Cheaper than olive oil, avocado oil, coconut oil, beef tallow, or butter. So almost every mainstream brand uses them, and almost every brand that wants to look healthy for less money uses them too.

You end up with a cart full of products that look fine on the front and are quietly inflammatory on the back.

Most of the advice people get about this does not work in a real grocery store.

"Shop the perimeter" — the perimeter has seed oils too. Rotisserie chickens are basted in them. Deli meats contain them. Pre-cut vegetables in bags sometimes have them in the dressing packet.

"Buy organic" — organic is a farming certification, not a seed oil exemption. Organic canola oil is still canola oil. You can have organic Doritos.

"Just avoid processed food" — fine in theory. Then you walk into a store with two kids in the cart and forty minutes to get dinner figured out.

"Read every label" — nobody does this. You would be in the store for three hours and you would still miss things.

None of these survive a real trip to the store. They assume you have unlimited time, a pristine budget, and no kids asking for fruit snacks.

## Six words. Ten seconds. That is the whole scan

You do not need to read every label. You need to scan for six words.

Canola. Soybean. Corn. Sunflower. Safflower. Cottonseed.

Those are the six most common seed oils in packaged food. Grapeseed and rice bran round out the list but they are rarer. If any of the six words appear in the first five ingredients, the product goes back on the shelf. No reading, no deliberating, no math.

The six words do not change. Once you have them memorised, the skill is yours. You will start to see the same brands over and over on the "put back" side and the same brands on the "buy" side. The store stops feeling like a minefield.

Here is how to use it this week:

1. Memorise the six words today. Canola, soybean, corn, sunflower, safflower, cottonseed. Say them out loud until they stick. It takes about a minute.

2. On your next grocery trip, pick three categories you buy every week. Chips, crackers, salad dressing are a good starting three. For each category, flip over the products you normally reach for and run the scan. If a seed oil is in the first five ingredients, put it back and pick up the next brand on the shelf. Primal Kitchen, Simple Mills, Siete, Mary's Gone Crackers, and Chosen Foods are the ones I land on most often. Most of them are at Walmart now, not just Whole Foods.

3. The week after, add two more categories. Mayo and cooking oil are the highest-impact next moves because they go into almost everything you make at home. Replace vegetable oil with olive, avocado, tallow, or butter. Replace mainstream mayo with a Primal Kitchen avocado oil version. That is it.

The front of the package is a sales pitch. The back is a contract. Learn to read the contract, and eighty percent of the trap on that shelf disappears.
