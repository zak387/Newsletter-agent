The Protein Halo

Kyle here.

Protein is the word every brand on the shelf is hiding behind right now. I do not trust the trend. I trust the back of the package.

I have been saying this for a while. Be skeptical of the protein trend. Eat beef, eggs, and real food. The last few months have put real numbers behind that, and they are worse than I expected. Lead in the tubs. Candy bars with the word protein on the front. Guidelines telling you to eat more of a thing most of you already get enough of.

Three stories this week that actually change what goes in my cart. Then the rest of the aisle in one pass.

**Today:**

- Two out of three protein powders tested came back high for lead
- The fastest growing claim on the shelf is now "high protein" and most of what is wearing it is junk
- The people writing the new guidelines told you to eat more protein. The nutritionists are pushing back.

---

### Two out of three protein powders tested came back high for lead

- **TL;DR:** A 2025 Consumer Reports investigation of 23 popular protein powders and shakes found that more than two-thirds of products exceeded 0.5 micrograms of lead per serving, with average lead levels higher than fifteen years ago, according to [Consumer Reports](https://www.consumerreports.org/lead/protein-powders-and-shakes-contain-high-levels-of-lead-a4206364640/).

**Three things to know**
- Plant-based powders were the worst. On average, nine times the lead of whey, and twice the lead of beef-based powders. The pea and rice blends sold as the clean option are not the clean option.
- A separate [Clean Label Project report](https://cleanlabelproject.org/protein-study-2-0/) found 47% of powders exceeded at least one California Prop 65 heavy metal limit. 21% came in at more than double the limit.
- This is not a sweetener story. This is the protein itself. You cannot flip the tub and swap the sucralose and fix this. The contamination is in the ingredient the brand is selling you.

**The verdict**

I use one powder. Grass-fed whey isolate, no sweeteners, no oils, no "natural flavors". That is the whole list. If the ingredient panel on your tub has more than five things on it, put it back this week.

And if your powder is pea, rice, or a plant blend, the lead data is not a maybe. It is an average. Switch to whey or beef, or drop the powder and hit your protein with real food. The cheapest complete protein in the store is still eggs. One ingredient. No label to read.

### The fastest growing claim on the shelf is now "high protein" and most of what is wearing it is junk

- **TL;DR:** Protein has moved from sports powders and gym bars into biscuits, cereals, brownies, ice cream, coffee, and candy-style bars, and brands can now command a 12% price premium for a "protein-rich" claim, according to [FoodNavigator-USA](https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2026/03/12/protein-snacks-and-ultra-processed-foods-the-growing-paradox-in-high-protein-food-innovation/).

**Three things to know**
- A candy bar with twelve grams of protein is still a candy bar. The protein does not delete the enriched wheat flour, the seed oils, or the sugar alcohols sitting underneath it.
- The brands doing most of the reformulation are Danone, PepsiCo, and General Mills. These are the same companies that sold you low-fat in the nineties. Same play. New word on the front.
- "High protein" is now doing the same job "natural" did ten years ago and "low-fat" did thirty years ago. It is a sales pitch. The front of the package is a sales pitch. The back is a contract.

**The verdict**

This is the biggest shift in the aisle since low-fat. And it is the same trap. Take a junk food. Bolt a health word on the front. Charge twelve percent more. Hope nobody turns it over.

Turn it over. If the first five ingredients look like a cookie, it is a cookie. Eat the cookie if you want the cookie. Just do not let a brand tell you a cookie is breakfast because it has twelve grams of pea protein in it.

Bad, better, best on this category: bad is a protein cookie or protein cereal. Better is a clean jerky like Chomps or a single-ingredient beef stick. Best is eggs, dairy, or meat from the actual meat section. That is the whole decision.

### The people writing the new guidelines said to eat more protein. The nutritionists are pushing back.

- **TL;DR:** The new 2025-2030 Dietary Guidelines push higher protein intake, and a January 2026 Rep Data survey found 86% of Americans are actively trying to eat more protein — but Harvard's Nutrition Source and FactCheck.org say protein has been "oversold, overvalued and overhyped" and most Americans already exceed the RDA, according to [Harvard's Nutrition Source](https://nutritionsource.hsph.harvard.edu/2026/01/09/dietary-guidelines-for-americans-2025-2030/) and [FactCheck.org](https://www.factcheck.org/2026/02/americans-dont-need-dramatically-more-protein-despite-officials-claims/).

**Three things to know**
- Men 19 to 59 already significantly exceed the protein RDA, mostly from meat, poultry, and eggs. The gap the guidelines are talking about is small and does not apply to most of the people panic-buying protein bars.
- The issue the nutritionists flagged is not protein itself. It is that the "more protein" message will be used to sell ultra-processed food with a health halo on it. Exactly what is already happening in the snack aisle.
- The RDA is 0.8g per kg of bodyweight. Do the math. Most of you are already there before you open a single tub.

**The verdict**

You probably do not have a protein problem. You have an ingredient problem.

If you eat eggs, meat, and dairy in a normal week, you are already hitting protein. The question is not how to add more. The question is what is riding along with it. Seed oils. Sucralose. Soy protein isolate wrapped around enriched wheat flour. That is the problem worth solving.

Stop counting grams. Start reading ingredients.

## Quick hits on the rest of the protein aisle

- Plant-based "chicken" and "beef" — seed oils, methylcellulose, soy protein isolate, and now worse lead numbers than whey. Real food is winning for a reason. Put it back.
- Protein chips — same chip, bolted-on pea protein, same seed oil frying base. Not a swap.
- Protein yogurt — check for sucralose and modified corn starch before you celebrate the twenty grams.
- Collagen — one ingredient is fine. Flavoured collagen is where the sweeteners sneak back in. Flip the tub.
- Jerky and beef sticks — the cleanest grab-and-go protein in the store if you read the back. Chomps and Mighty Jerky are the defaults I grab.

---

Three reads this week. Pull every product in your house with "protein" on the front of the package and flip it over. If the back does not match the promise on the front, find the one that does. Progress over perfection.
